How to run a 1:1 meeting well is one of those things nobody actually teaches you. You become a manager, someone tells you to do weekly 1:1s, and you figure out the rest on your own. Which means most managers default to the easiest version: a calendar invite, a vague "how's it going," a quick status rundown, and out.

That's not a 1:1. That's a check-in with a fancy name.

The difference matters because a 1:1 done right is the single highest-leverage conversation you have as a manager. It's the only dedicated time to build real trust, catch problems early, and actually develop the person sitting across from you. Done wrong, or not done at all, it's 30 minutes of mutual performance where nothing of substance gets said.

Here's how to run one that earns its spot on the calendar.

## What a 1:1 is actually for (hint, it's not a status update)

The first thing to get clear on: the 1:1 belongs to your direct report, not to you.

I know that sounds obvious. But watch what most managers do in 1:1s, they run through what they need, share updates about things happening above the team, and ask clarifying questions about work they want visibility into. That's a manager-centered meeting.

A direct-report-centered meeting asks different questions. What do *they* need from you this week? What's making their job harder? What's going well that they want you to know about? What's worrying them that they haven't said out loud yet?

The job of a 1:1 isn't to get you information. It's to give your direct report a safe, structured space where real conversation can happen. Status updates have Slack, standups, and project boards. The 1:1 has no other substitute.

## How often should 1:1s happen?

Weekly, unless there's a real reason not to.

Some managers go biweekly to "reduce calendar noise." That's almost always a mistake. A lot happens in two weeks. Problems sit longer. Momentum fades. The relationship gets thinner.

Weekly 1:1s feel like a lot until you run them well: then they become the part of your week that makes everything else easier. You catch things before they become crises. You spend less time in reactive mode.

For new team members or anyone going through a difficult stretch, consider twice a week. For very senior, autonomous people, biweekly might work. But weekly is the right default.

Keep them to 30 minutes for most people. Some relationships benefit from 45 or 60 minutes, but start at 30 and expand if needed. A focused 30-minute 1:1 beats a meandering 60-minute one every time.

## A simple agenda format that works

Here's the structure I come back to:

**1. Open with their stuff (10–15 min)**
Start by asking what's on their mind. Not "how are things going?": that's too easy to brush off. Ask something more direct: "What's your biggest priority this week?" or "What do you need to get off your plate that we can talk through?"

Let them lead this part. Don't jump in to solve things too fast. Listen to what's under the surface, not just what they're saying.

**2. Feedback and development (10 min)**
This is where most managers skip to status updates instead. Don't. Use this time for something that actually helps them grow: give specific feedback (positive or constructive), ask about their goals, or discuss a skill they're working on.

You don't need a big feedback moment every week, sometimes it's a quick observation, "I noticed how you handled that client call and wanted to say that was sharp." That takes 30 seconds and it matters.

**3. Your stuff (5 min)**
Save the last few minutes for what you need from them. Context they should have, decisions that need input, or things you want to flag. But keep this secondary. If you front-load the meeting with what you need, you signal that this is your meeting, not theirs.

## Questions worth asking in every 1:1

You don't need to ask all of these. Pick one or two that fit the moment.

- What's gotten in your way most this week?
- What's something you're working on that you're proud of?
- Is there anything you're unsure about that I can clarify?
- What would make next week easier than this one?
- Is there anything about how we're working together that you'd change?
- Where do you feel like you're growing? Where do you feel stuck?
- What's one thing I could do more or less of as your manager?

That last one is uncomfortable to ask. Ask it anyway. The feedback you get from it is usually more useful than anything you'd come up with on your own.

## What to do when the 1:1 goes flat

It happens. Some weeks the conversation is easy. Other weeks it feels like pulling teeth. Here's what to do when it stalls:

**Change the location.** Walk-and-talks break stale patterns. Even moving from a conference room to a coffee area helps. Different setting, different energy.

**Ask more specific questions.** "How are things going?" gets you "fine." "What's the one thing on your list you keep pushing to tomorrow?" gets you something real.

**Name the dynamic.** If someone consistently gives you short answers, say it directly: "I've noticed our 1:1s can feel a little surface-level: I want to make sure this time is actually useful for you. What would make it better?" Most people will tell you if you ask.

**Check if you're creating safety.** People don't open up if they think what they say will be used against them. If you've reacted badly to honesty in the past, that follows you into the next 1:1 and the one after that. The fix is a track record of handling hard conversations well: which takes time to build.

## The one thing most managers skip

Consistency.

The best 1:1 you can run is a mediocre one that happens every single week. Regularity builds the relationship. When you cancel constantly or skip weeks because you're busy, you signal that the meeting, and by extension the person: isn't a priority.

It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to happen. Protect the time.
