There was someone on my team who was consistently late to our Monday standups. Not by five minutes. By fifteen, sometimes twenty. Every week. The rest of the team noticed. I noticed. I talked about it with exactly one person: my own manager, in a one-line vent during a 1:1, which amounted to nothing.

I kept waiting for it to fix itself. I told myself he was dealing with something personal. I told myself the standups probably started too early. I told myself I'd bring it up soon, once I had more context, once things settled down a bit.

This went on for two months.

What finally broke it was a team member, Priya, who pulled me aside after a Thursday afternoon meeting and said, carefully, "I just want you to know that a few of us have been talking about the Monday thing. It feels like it's okay because nobody's said anything." She wasn't accusing me of anything. She was telling me the truth about what silence communicates to a team.

I had the conversation with him the next day. He apologized, explained what was going on, and we worked out a different check-in format for a few weeks while he sorted through it. It took twenty minutes.

Two months of friction. Twenty minutes to address it.

I've replayed that situation more than once. Not because it ended badly, but because I can still feel the specific discomfort of knowing I needed to say something and choosing not to.

## The Cost of Waiting Is Not Zero

Most new managers avoid difficult conversations the same way I did: by reframing the situation until the urgency fades. "It's probably temporary." "I don't want to seem harsh." "Maybe I'm being too sensitive about it." These aren't irrational thoughts. They're normal. But they have a compounding cost that's easy to underestimate.

The longer you wait, the more context the other person is missing. They're operating without feedback that would actually help them. Meanwhile, the issue is visible to the rest of the team, and your silence is sending a signal whether you intend it to or not.

Kim Scott, who wrote *Radical Candor*, calls the pattern of staying quiet to protect someone's feelings "ruinous empathy." The framing stuck with me. It's not kind to withhold honest feedback. It just feels kinder in the moment. The person ends up blindsided later, either in a performance review, or when the situation escalates past the point where a conversation fixes it.

> "Silence isn't neutral. When your team is watching how you handle something difficult, your non-response is the response."

The hard truth is that most of what feels like "protecting" someone is actually protecting yourself from a few minutes of discomfort. I've been guilty of this. I'm not sure it ever fully goes away, but it helps to name it.

## Why the First Sentence Is the Hardest Part

A lot of managers I've talked to don't struggle with what to say once a hard conversation is underway. The thing that stops them is starting. The conversation lives in the future, as a mental object, and it's always slightly worse in your imagination than in practice.

The fix is having a reliable opening. Not a script, but a structure. If you know how the first thirty seconds will go, the rest is easier to navigate.

Here's what works for me: be direct about the purpose of the conversation without editorializing about it. Something like: "I want to talk about something I should have brought up sooner. It's about the Monday standups. I've noticed you've been late consistently, and I should have said something before now."

That's it. No softening preamble that buries the actual topic. No starting with compliments to pad the news. Just a clear statement of what the conversation is about and an honest acknowledgment that you're raising it a little late.

**Common mistake:** Opening with something so vague the person doesn't know what you're actually talking about until three minutes in. "I've been thinking about team dynamics and how things have been going" is not a conversation opener. It's anxiety dressed up as structure. The person can feel something difficult coming, they just don't know what it is, which makes the discomfort worse for both of you.

What you're aiming for is clarity, not comfort. Clarity is actually kinder.

## How to Prepare Without Over-Preparing

I've seen managers go in one of two directions on preparation: they either wing it completely (and regret it), or they spend so long preparing that they never actually have the conversation. I've done both.

The useful version of preparation takes about fifteen minutes and covers three things:

**1. The observable facts.** What specifically happened, when, how many times. Not your interpretation of what it means, not your theory about why. Just the concrete thing you observed. "In the last six weeks, you've started our Monday standup more than fifteen minutes late on four occasions" is a fact. "It seems like you don't respect the team's time" is an interpretation. Start with facts.

**2. The impact.** How is this affecting the team, the work, or you? This is where you can be honest about what you've noticed. "When you're not there at the start, we either wait or we catch you up, and either way we lose time" is an impact statement. It's not an accusation. It's cause and effect.

**3. What you're asking for.** What do you need to change? What does a better version of this situation look like? If you go in without a clear ask, you're more likely to end the conversation with a vague agreement that doesn't actually lead anywhere.

Claire Hughes Johnson, in *Scaling People*, describes the importance of separating observation from judgment in feedback conversations. She's talking about performance at scale, but the principle applies here: the cleaner you can keep the factual layer from the interpretive layer, the less defensive the conversation gets.

**Try this week:** Before any conversation you've been putting off, write down one observable fact, one impact statement, and one specific ask. Not a script, just those three lines. You might not even need to look at them during the conversation. The act of writing them down forces the clarity that most avoidance conversations are missing.

## When It Gets Uncomfortable (and It Will)

The conversation is going fine, and then it shifts. Maybe they get defensive. Maybe they bring up something you weren't expecting. Maybe they get emotional in a way that makes you want to wrap up fast and escape.

This is where most difficult conversations go sideways, not because anything terrible happened, but because the manager rushes to resolution too quickly. The discomfort spikes, they feel the urge to smooth it over, and they end with something like "I'm sure it'll be fine" when that's not actually what they think.

Sitting with discomfort in a conversation is a skill. It takes practice. The practical version is giving people a moment to respond without immediately filling the silence. If someone goes quiet after you say something honest, that silence doesn't need to be fixed. They're processing. Let them.

If they get defensive, reflect back what you heard before you respond. "It sounds like this caught you off guard" or "I hear you that this feels unfair" acknowledges what they said without agreeing with it or abandoning the point you need to make. It keeps the conversation from becoming a standoff.

I'm not sure I always get this right. I still feel the pull to smooth things over before they're actually resolved. The thing I've gotten better at is noticing when I'm doing it.

> "You're not trying to win the conversation. You're trying to reach something honest. Those require different approaches."

## After the Conversation: The Part Everyone Skips

The conversation itself is the hard part. What comes after is easy to neglect.

Two things matter here. One is follow-up. If you agreed on something, check in on it. A brief mention in your next 1:1 -- "wanted to see how the new schedule is working for you" -- closes the loop and signals that you took it seriously. Without that, the conversation can feel like it happened in a vacuum.

The other is how you show up in the days after. Some managers get awkward. They overcorrect toward warmth in a way that feels performative, or they get distant because they're not sure how to act. The right call is just to be normal. Treat the person the same way you did before. If you had a hard conversation with someone and then started acting differently around them, it makes the conversation feel like a rupture instead of a routine part of working together.

One of my former managers said something I've tried to hold onto: "You don't want your team to be afraid of feedback conversations. That means you can't act like they're rare or significant. They have to be part of the rhythm." She was right. The more normal you make these conversations, the less each one carries.

**Try this week:** Think of one conversation you've been putting off for more than a week. Not the hardest one on your list -- just one. Write down the three-line prep (fact, impact, ask), and schedule ten minutes this week to have it. Start with something lower-stakes if you need to build the habit. The goal isn't to get it perfect. The goal is to stop waiting.

## It Gets Easier. Slowly.

I want to be honest about this: the discomfort doesn't disappear. There are still conversations I put off longer than I should. There are still times I feel the pull toward avoidance and have to push through it anyway.

What changes, over time, is your evidence base. You remember the last difficult conversation you had and how it was better than you expected. You remember the situation where you stayed quiet and it made things worse. You build a track record that you can refer to when the anxiety shows up.

The first few times are the hardest. After that, you're not starting from zero.

I built Cadence partly because I kept losing track of the things I needed to say. A note in one app, a mental reminder I forgot, an email I flagged and never came back to. The conversations that mattered, and the follow-ups that were supposed to happen, kept slipping. The right structure doesn't make hard conversations easier, but it does make sure they actually happen. That's a start.
